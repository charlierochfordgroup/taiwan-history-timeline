# History of Taiwan – Comprehensive Timeline

---

## Prehistory & Early Settlement

- **450,000–190,000 years ago** – Partial jawbone (Penghu 1) found in Taiwan Strait channel suggests a previously unknown species of *Homo* inhabited the area when sea levels were ~140m lower and the strait was a land bridge
- **~20,000–30,000 years ago** – Oldest evidence of modern human presence on Taiwan: bone fragments and a tooth found at Zuojhen District
- **~15,000–5,000 years ago** – Changbin culture (Paleolithic chipped-pebble tools) found in Taitung; same culture at Eluanbi on the southern tip
- **~11,000 years ago** – Evidence of slash-and-burn agriculture at Sun Moon Lake (pollen/spore analysis)
- **~10,000 years ago** – Sea levels rise at the start of the Holocene, forming the Taiwan Strait and cutting Taiwan off from the Asian mainland
- **~8,000 years ago** – Liangdao Man skeleton found on Liang Island (Matsu archipelago)
- **~3,000 BC** – Neolithic Dapenkeng culture appears abruptly and spreads around the coast; characterised by corded-ware pottery, polished stone adzes, rice and millet cultivation. Believed to be ancestors of today's Taiwanese aborigines, speaking early Austronesian languages
- **Post-3,000 BC** – Some Dapenkeng descendants migrate from Taiwan to Southeast Asia and across the Pacific/Indian Oceans, making Taiwan the origin point of the Austronesian language family (Malayo-Polynesian is just one branch; all other branches are found only on Taiwan)
- **~400 AD** – Wrought iron being produced locally using bloomeries, technology possibly introduced from the Philippines

---

## Chinese Contact & Early Settlement (~230 AD – 1620s)

- **230 AD** – Sun Quan, emperor of the Wu dynasty (Three Kingdoms period), sends troops to an island called Yizhou (possibly Taiwan). They take several thousand captives and return to China; the island is subsequently deemed "barbarian territory" and not worth China's attention
- **Early 7th century** – Emperor Yang of the Sui dynasty sends three expeditions to a place called "Liuqiu" (possibly Taiwan or Ryukyu Islands)
- **1171** – Chinese fishermen settled on the Penghu Islands near Taiwan. "Bisheye" raiders (Taiwanese people related to the Bisaya of the Philippines) land on Penghu and plunder Chinese fields; Song dynasty sends soldiers and begins regular patrols
- **1292** – Yuan dynasty emperor Kublai Khan sends officials to demand loyalty; they end up in Taiwan, mistake it for Ryukyu. Three soldiers are killed; the delegation retreats
- **1349** – Wang Dayuan visits Taiwan and provides the first detailed written description of the island, noting the presence of Chinese trade pottery but no Chinese settlers (many on Penghu, however)
- **Early 1400s** – Ming navy at its pinnacle with over 3,500 vessels, ~2,700 of which are warships stationed along the Chinese coastline
- **1500s** – Taiwan used as a base by Japanese and Chinese merchants and pirates; increasing numbers of Chinese fishermen, traders, and pirates visit the southwestern coast
- **1542** – Portuguese sailors note Taiwan on their maps as *Ilha Formosa* ("beautiful island")
- **1582** – Portuguese shipwreck survivors spend 45 days on Taiwan battling malaria and aborigines
- **Late 1500s** – Chinese from Fujian begin settling in southwestern Taiwan. Ming officials start issuing licences for trade in northern Taiwan (1593), acknowledging already-existing illegal trade
- **1603** – Chen Di visits Taiwan on an anti-pirate expedition; writes *Dongfanji* ("An Account of the Eastern Barbarians"), one of the earliest detailed accounts of Taiwan's native peoples. Notes substantial Chinese settlers already living alongside the indigenous population
- **By 1623** – When the Dutch arrive, they find ~1,500–2,000 Chinese visitors and residents in two villages (one of which would become Tainan), plus a Chinese community living in an aboriginal village speaking a creole language

---

## Dutch & Spanish Colonial Period (1624–1662)

- **1624** – Dutch East India Company (VOC) builds Fort Zeelandia on the islet of Tayowan off the southwest coast, after being driven from Penghu by Ming authorities. This becomes their main base
- **1625** – Dutch buy land from the Sinkan village and build the town of Sakam for Dutch and Chinese merchants
- **1626** – Spanish establish a settlement at Santísima Trinidad in northeastern Taiwan (modern Keelung), building Fort San Salvador, as a counter to Dutch presence
- **1624–25** – Li Tan ("Captain China") oversees a fleet of pirate ships while based in Taiwan
- **1629** – Spanish build Fort Santo Domingo at Tamsui. Mattau and Soulang warriors kill 60 Dutch musketeers in an ambush
- **1633** – Zheng Zhilong (Koxinga's father) defeats Dutch fleet in sea battle near Quanzhou; Dutch subsequently reconcile with Zheng, who arranges more Chinese trade in Taiwan
- **1635–36** – Dutch defeat Mattau and Baccluan; pacify multiple aboriginal villages. Dutch and allies massacre inhabitants of Liuqiu Island (sealing ~300 in caves, killing with poisonous fumes; population of 1,100 removed, men enslaved, women and children distributed)
- **1636** – Favorolang (largest aboriginal village north of Mattau) kills three Chinese – beginning of years of conflict
- **1638** – Dutch build Fort Provintia on the main island's harbour shore
- **Late 1630s** – VOC encourages mass Chinese migration to Taiwan; advertises economic benefits, even pays them to move. Thousands arrive as rice and sugar planters
- **1642** – Dutch seize Spanish fortress at Keelung; Spanish and Filipino garrisons disperse or retreat to the Philippines. Dutch now control both north and south
- **1650** – Dutch estimate ~50,000 natives in the western plains; administer ~315 tribal villages with a total population of ~68,600
- **1652** – Guo Huaiyi rebellion: Chinese farmer leads army of peasants against Dutch over oppressive taxation. Suppressed with ~4,000 Chinese killed. Rural labour force devastated
- **Dutch legacy** – Introduced romanised script of Formosan languages, Christianity, a unified tax system, schools, and the ho-ko mutual responsibility system. All later adopted by succeeding rulers

---

## Koxinga & the Kingdom of Tungning (1661–1683)

- **1646** – Zheng Zhilong (Koxinga's father) defects to the Manchu Qing; is captured and taken to Beijing in chains. His son Koxinga (Cheng Cheng-kung / Zheng Chenggong) burns his scholar's robes and takes up the Ming loyalist cause
- **1646–59** – Koxinga rebuilds his father's pirate fleet and army; establishes a major Ming outpost along China's south coast in Fujian. At his peak: ~50,000 cavalry, 70,000 infantry, 10,000 elite "iron men," and a fleet of over 3,000 junks
- **1659** – Koxinga besieges Nanjing with ~170,000 men; on the verge of victory, he throws a birthday celebration and his drunken army is routed by a Manchu cavalry night sortie. Retreats to Xiamen with only 400 battered junks
- **April 1661** – Koxinga launches invasion of Taiwan with 25,000 soldiers on 400 war junks from Kinmen; receives ecstatic welcome from Chinese settlers on the island
- **May 1661** – Dutch warship *Hector* explodes and sinks in battle. Fort Provintia surrenders to Koxinga
- **February 1662** – After a nine-month siege, Fort Zeelandia surrenders. Dutch colonial rule ends (though they hold out at Keelung until 1668)
- **Koxinga renames** Fort Zeelandia to Anping, Fort Provintia to Chikan. Establishes one prefecture and two counties
- **June 1662** – Koxinga dies at age 37. Chinese nationalists in the 20th century invoke him for his patriotism and loyalty against the Qing and Western influence
- **1662–81** – Koxinga's son Zheng Jing rules. Establishes Ming-style administration with Six Ministries, Confucian Shrine, Imperial Academy, and civil service examinations. Dispatches teachers to aboriginal tribes; expansion of Chinese settlements comes at the expense of aboriginal land, causing rebellions. Chinese population more than doubles
- **1674–80** – Zheng Jing joins the Revolt of the Three Feudatories; recaptures territory on the mainland coast but is eventually forced back to Taiwan after a series of defeats
- **1681** – Zheng Jing dies; political turmoil as his son Zheng Keshuang murders his brother. Multiple followers defect to the Qing
- **July 1683** – Shi Lang leads Qing fleet of 238 ships and 21,000+ men against Penghu; decisive naval battle in which ~12,000 Zheng forces perish. Penghu falls
- **October 1683** – Zheng Keshuang surrenders; shaves his head in Manchu style. Kingdom of Tungning ends
- **Tungning legacy** – By end of Zheng rule, cultivated area had tripled since the Dutch era; sugar exports exceeded Dutch figures; greater economic diversification achieved; government extracting 30%+ more annual silver income than the Dutch in 1655

## Qing Dynasty Rule (1683–1895)

### Annexation & Early Administration
- **1684** – After debate (some officials advocated abandoning Taiwan entirely), Kangxi Emperor accepts Shi Lang's proposal to annex Taiwan. Established as Taiwan Prefecture, a sub-unit of Fujian Province
- **Qing initially forbids** mainlanders from moving to Taiwan; sends most people back to the mainland. Official population only 50,000. But illegal migrants number tens of thousands yearly by 1711
- **Permit system** – Migrants barred from bringing families (to ensure they would return). Resulted in the idiom: "has Tangshan father, no Tangshan mother." Overwhelmingly male migrants married locally, including aboriginal women
- **1722** – Boundary markers (54 stelae) erected to segregate Chinese settlers from aboriginal territory. Rebuilt in 1750, 1760, 1784, and 1790 as encroachment continued regardless

### Settler Expansion & Rebellions
- **1721** – Zhu Yigui rebellion: anti-Qing uprising sparked by increased taxation. Rebels capture all of Qing-held Taiwan in less than two weeks. Crushed by Qing fleet; Zhu executed
- **1786–88** – Lin Shuangwen rebellion: Tiandihui (Heaven and Earth Society) members break jailed members free. Large-scale uprising involving new arrivals who could not find land. Lin attacks Changhua County, kills 2,000 civilians. Eventually suppressed by 60,000 Qing troops; Lin executed
- **Over 200 years of Qing rule** – Taiwan described as in "a state of constant rebellion." Most rebellions caused by Han settler disputes, not aboriginal resistance. Major uprisings every few years; ethnic tensions between Hoklo and Hakka settlers add to instability
- **By late Qing** – Western plains fully developed with ~2.5 million Chinese inhabitants. Mountain areas still largely autonomous aboriginal territory

### Foreign Incursions
- **1840s** – British suggest seizing Taiwan during the First Opium War. In 1841 British try to scale heights around Keelung harbour three times but fail. Shipwreck survivors (over 130 from the *Nerbudda*, 54 from the *Ann*) are executed in Tainan in 1842
- **1867** – American barque *Rover* shipwrecks off southern Taiwan; captain and crew killed by Paiwan aborigines. US Consul Le Gendre negotiates with aboriginal chief Tanketok
- **1871** – Ryukyuan sailors shipwreck on southeastern Taiwan; 54 killed by aborigines (the Mudan Incident)
- **1874** – Japan invades southern Taiwan using the Mudan Incident as pretext; 3,600 Japanese troops occupy indigenous territory for six months. 561 Japanese die (mostly of illness). Qing pays indemnity; Japan withdraws but gains recognition of Ryukyu as its vassal

### Modernisation Attempts
- **1875** – Ban on entering Taiwan lifted. Imperial commissioner Shen Baozhen recommends subjugating aborigines and populating their territory with Chinese to prevent further Japanese encroachment
- **1884–85** – Sino-French War: French invade northern Taiwan (Keelung Campaign). Capture Keelung but cannot advance beyond its perimeter. French blockade Taiwan. Eventually withdraw after agreement
- **1885** – Taiwan elevated to a full province (previously a prefecture of Fujian). Governor Liu Mingchuan launches modernisation: telegraph lines, railway (Keelung–Taipei–Hsinchu), electric lighting, modern weaponry, steamship service
- **1885–91** – Liu launches 40+ military campaigns against aborigines with 17,500 soldiers; a third of the invasion force killed or disabled. Campaigns largely unsuccessful despite modern weapons (Gatling guns, mortars, land mines). Liu declares victory in a memorandum to Beijing but in reality achieves little
- **Railway** – Plagued by corruption, incompetence, and sabotage. Soldiers used surveying pegs as firewood; officers diverted routes for bribes. Tunnels drilled from opposite sides met with a 14-foot height difference. Japanese later rebuilt much of it from scratch
- **1891** – Liu resigns due to political opposition and recurring malaria; modernisation efforts largely cease under his weak successor

### Cession to Japan
- **April 1895** – Treaty of Shimonoseki: after losing the First Sino-Japanese War, Qing cedes Taiwan and Penghu to Japan "in perpetuity." Loss of Taiwan becomes a rallying point for Chinese nationalism

## Republic of Formosa (May–October 1895)

- **May 16, 1895** – Civic leaders led by Hakka gentry member Ch'iu Feng-chia pressure Governor T'ang Ching-sung to declare independence
- **May 23** – Taiwan Republic formally declared in Taipei. Declaration says "the People of Taiwan are determined to die rather than be subdued by the Japanese." Tiger flag on blue background unfurled (remains a potent symbol for Taiwanese independence advocates)
- **May 25** – T'ang inaugurated as president. Republic is "suzerain" to the Qing – essentially semi-independent, acknowledging only general overlordship by Beijing ("one country, two systems, perhaps" – Manthorpe)
- **May 29** – Japanese Imperial Guard lands at Aoli, southeast of Keelung
- **June 3** – Keelung falls after a day's hard fighting. Defence is shambolic: militia units under independent gentry warlords refuse to coordinate
- **June 5** – President T'ang flees on a German passenger ship. Shore batteries fire at the ship as it steams out of Tamsui harbour
- **Ch'iu Feng-chia** also leaves for the mainland; never returns to Taiwan
- **June 7** – Japanese enter Taipei unopposed after Guangdong warriors go on a looting rampage that kills at least 500 people. Taipei merchants invite the Japanese in to restore order
- **September–October** – Resistance stiffens in the south. Black Flag Liu Yung-fu forms a temporary government in Tainan; issues money and postage stamps
- **October 21** – Tainan handed over without fighting after Liu flees in disguise on a British ship
- **Not one of the European powers or the United States ever recognised the Taiwan Republic**
- **Between 200,000 and 300,000 people flee Taiwan** in 1895. From 1895 to 1897, ~6,400 sell their property and leave
- **Significance** – The republic lasted only five months, but it told the Taiwanese that their fate was in their own hands: when their future was in the balance, both China and the great powers deserted them

## Japanese Colonial Rule (1895–1945)

### Resistance & Pacification (1895–1915)
- **1895–1902** – Five months of sustained warfare after the invasion, followed by partisan attacks until 1902. At least 12,000 "bandit-rebels" killed (in addition to 6,000–14,000 killed in the initial resistance war)
- **June 1896** – 6,000 Taiwanese slaughtered in the Yunlin Massacre
- **1895–1920** – At least 8,200 Taiwanese arrested each year for alleged attempts to overthrow Japanese rule. 19 major uprisings during the entire period of Japanese rule, including 2 attempts to take Taipei
- **1907** – Beipu uprising: Hakka and Saisiyat insurgents kill 57 Japanese; 100 Hakka men and boys killed in reprisal
- **1913** – Luo Fuxing (Tongmenghui member) plans a rebellion; over 1,000 Taiwanese executed by Japanese police
- **1915** – Tapani Incident: Yu Qingfang's religious group defies Japanese authority. 1,413 captured; Yu and 200 followers executed. Military police lure additional anti-Japanese militants out with a pardon, then execute them in a field. Oral tradition suggests 5,000–6,000 killed

### Colonial Administration & Development
- **1898** – Goto Shimpei appointed chief civil administrator. Implements "biological colonial management" – work with Taiwanese customs first, then gradually reshape them. Creates omnipresent police force (~12,000 officers + 5,000 in aboriginal areas) and revives ho-ko (mutual responsibility) system. Taiwan becomes a thorough police state
- **Japan brings** unified weights and measures, centralised banking (Bank of Taiwan, 1897), education, rail network connecting north and south, dam-supported irrigation (55% of agricultural land), ports, health care system
- **Agricultural transformation** – Food production increases fourfold; sugar cane production increases 15-fold between 1895 and 1925. Taiwan becomes a major food basket for Japan's economy
- **Average lifespan** reaches 60 years by 1945 (up from much lower under Qing)
- **Most Taiwanese felt their lives had improved** under Japanese rule compared to previous Chinese rule, despite the repression

### Aboriginal Policies
- **1903** – Government implements harsher policies toward aborigines: expands guard lines, restricts living space. Sakuma Samata launches five-year plan using landmines and electrified fences
- **1911** – Army invades mountainous areas; by 1915 many aboriginal villages destroyed. By 1903, indigenous rebellions had already killed 1,900 Japanese
- **1919–34** – Aborigines relocated; allotted 3 hectares of reserve land each, later reduced. Given less than half the originally promised land, or one-eighth of ancestral lands
- **1930** – Musha (Wushe) Uprising: last major aboriginal rebellion. Seediq warriors led by Mona Rudao attack police stations and a school; ~134 Japanese killed. Armed conflict ends in December when Seediq leaders commit suicide
- **Total casualties** in aboriginal conflicts: ~4,422 civilians dead, ~2,660 military killed, ~4,160 wounded

### Non-Violent Resistance & Political Movements
- **1921** – Chiang Wei-shui founds the Taiwanese Cultural Association (TCA); promotes vernacular Chinese language. "Taiwan is Taiwan people's Taiwan" becomes common position for all anti-Japanese groups
- **1920** – Lin Hsien-tang and 178 Taiwanese file a petition seeking self-determination; rejected. Petitions continue annually
- **1923** – TCA co-founds Taiwan People's News (published in Tokyo, shipped to Taiwan, subjected to censorship). 99 Alliance members arrested for attempting to set up a parliament
- **1927** – TCA splits; factions include the Taiwanese People's Party and a subsidiary of the Taiwanese Communist Party (the only group advocating outright independence)
- **1931** – TPP terminated by authorities. Chiang Wei-shui dies

### Japanisation (1937–1945)
- **1937** – Full-scale war with China; kōminka (imperial Japanisation) project launched. Chinese language removed from newspapers and education; Taiwan's history erased from curriculum
- **1940** – Name-changing campaign: only 7% of Taiwanese adopt Japanese names by war's end
- **Comfort women** – Between 1,000–2,000 Taiwanese women forced into the system. Aboriginal women served Japanese military in mountain regions. More than half were minors, some as young as 14
- **1937–45** – Over 207,000 Taiwanese employed by Japanese military. ~50,000 go missing or die; 21 executed for war crimes
- **Taiwan as military base** – Used for invasion of Guangdong (1938), occupation of Hainan (1939), launch of southward advance after Pearl Harbor. Base for naval and air attacks on Luzon and rear staging for attacks on Myanmar
- **Late 1944** – Taiwan bombed in US air raids; estimated 16,000–30,000 civilians killed
- **1942** – Taiwan's real GDP per capita peaks at $1,522; declines to $693 by 1944 due to wartime destruction
- **End of war** – 40% of railroads unusable; 200+ factories bombed; 3 of 4 electrical power plants destroyed. ~350,000 Japanese civilians living in Taiwan

## Return of Chinese Rule & the White Terror (1945–1987)

### Handover & KMT Misrule
- **August 15, 1945** – Emperor Hirohito announces Japan's surrender. Taiwanese wait eagerly for liberation. Nothing happens for weeks – Japanese maintain order, police continue patrols
- **October 25, 1945** – General Chen Yi (appointed by Chiang Kai-shek) arrives and receives Japanese surrender. Proclaims Taiwan reincorporated into China. Allies object: the legal resolution awaits a formal peace treaty; KMT merely administers Taiwan on behalf of the Allies
- **Chen Yi establishes** a military administration staffed exclusively by mainlanders and his relatives. Systematic looting begins at every level: ordinary soldiers plunder (stories of soldiers carrying stolen bicycles on their backs because they didn't know how to ride); officers ship Japanese stores to the mainland for sale; Chen's coterie seize ~90% of the economy through monopolies
- **Economy collapses** – Consumer price index spirals: food prices rise 21,000+ points between November 1945 and January 1947. Rice crop falls to half of 1930s levels. Bubonic plague and cholera become endemic again as quinine stores are sold to the mainland
- **Wall posters**: "The dogs have gone and the pigs have arrived" and "At least the Japanese dogs protected property"

### The 228 Incident (February 28, 1947)
- **February 27, 1947** – Widow Lin Chiang-mai, selling cigarettes in a Taipei park, is accosted by Monopoly Commission agents. She is struck with a pistol butt; an agent fires into the crowd, killing one man
- **February 28** – Several thousand people march on commission offices and then the governor's office. Soldiers fire a machine gun into the crowd. Uprising spreads island-wide within two days. Martial law declared
- **March 1–7** – Civic leaders form a settlement committee; present 32 demands including end to military rule, Taiwan as an autonomous province, free elections, release of political prisoners. Chen rejects them
- **March 8** – 10,000 Kuomintang reinforcements land at Keelung, 3,000 at Kaohsiung. Troops begin campaign of indiscriminate shooting, raping, and bayoneting
- **Aftermath** – Government forces systematically hunt anyone considered a leader of Taiwanese society: lawyers, businessmen, newspaper editors, settlement committee members. Many tortured and mutilated before being killed, bodies left in streets. ~100 middle school students arbitrarily executed. **KMT's own records: at least 28,000 killed**
- **Chen Yi recalled** in March; promoted to governor of Zhejiang. In 1949, caught trying to defect to the Communists; executed by firing squad in Taiwan in June 1950 (news sparks a day of celebration across the island)

### Retreat to Taiwan & Martial Law
- **1949** – Communists win the Chinese Civil War. Soviet Union supported communists; US supported the KMT
- **December 1949** – Chiang Kai-shek formally moves the ROC capital to Taipei. ~2 million mainlanders flood to Taiwan (1.2 million in December alone), including 600,000 surviving KMT army members. Only ~6 million local Taiwanese on the island at this point
- **Chiang's plan**: regroup on Taiwan, then return to the mainland. "Prepare for one year, counter-attack in two years, sweep out the enemy in three years and succeed within five years"
- **Mainlanders dominate** government and civil services. KMT is exclusively mainlanders in the administration; not business-savvy; deeply unpopular
- **Martial law continues** from 1947 to 1987 – the "White Terror" period. Taiwan Garrison Command secret police ferret out dissidents. Opposition parties banned. Island governed as a one-party state with only token elections at the district level

### Economic Development
- **1949–53** – Land reform: rents capped at 37.5% of crop value; government sells confiscated Japanese farmland in family-sized lots; landlords required to sell excess holdings. At least 2 million Taiwanese become landowners; farmers' incomes double; productivity up 50% by 1963. Creates equity base for the later industrial revolution
- **1950–65** – Taiwan receives $1.5 billion in US economic aid and $2.4 billion in military aid. US advisers help frame land reform, infrastructure, and industrial policy. Aid is ~$100 million/year through the 1950s, representing a third of all capital investment
- **1953** – Inflation finally curbed by trimming money supply and high-interest savings deposits
- **1960s–70s** – Light industrial revolution: Taiwan becomes an export-driven "economic miracle" economy. Enterprising farmers-turned-entrepreneurs use land equity as collateral for bank loans
- **1963** – US shuts off economic aid tap; Taiwan is a going concern
- **Military spending** – Up to 12% of GDP, sustained by constant threat from mainland

### Cold War Context
- **January 1950** – President Truman announces US will have no further involvement in the Chinese civil war. Washington writes off Taiwan
- **June 25, 1950** – Korean War breaks out. Two days later Truman orders the 7th Fleet to patrol the Taiwan Strait (both to prevent Chinese invasion of Taiwan AND to prevent Chiang from invading the mainland). Taiwan is suddenly "the unsinkable aircraft carrier"
- **1951** – Japanese Peace Treaty signed in San Francisco: Japan formally relinquishes Taiwan but does not specify a recipient. The legal status of Taiwan remains ambiguous
- **1954** – US and ROC sign a defence treaty. US prohibits Kuomintang from unilateral offensive action against the mainland
- **1958** – Communist shelling of Kinmen and Mazu. US pressures Chiang to abandon some offshore islands; he refuses most
- **1960s** – Vietnam War: Taiwan used as a staging point for US bombing raids on North Vietnam and R&R centre for troops
- **1969** – Chiang orders advanced nuclear weapons program using a Canadian research reactor; later agrees to dismantle it under US pressure

## Democratisation (1971–2000)

### Opening to China & Loss of International Standing
- **1971** – US official position: Taiwan's status is "undetermined." PRC replaces ROC at the United Nations
- **1972** – Nixon visits China. Shanghai Communiqué: US "acknowledges" the Chinese position that Taiwan is part of China, but does not endorse it
- **1979** – US formally recognises Beijing (PRC) and derecognises Taipei (ROC). Congress passes Taiwan Relations Act, committing US to provide Taiwan with defensive weapons and maintain capacity to resist coercion
- **1979** – Kaohsiung Incident: pro-democracy demonstrators attacked by police; arrests and trials of opposition leaders galvanise Taiwanese civil society

### Transition to Democracy
- **April 1975** – Chiang Kai-shek dies. Succeeded by Yen Chia-kan as president; son Chiang Ching-kuo becomes KMT chairman and president in 1978
- **Chiang Ching-kuo** – formerly head of the feared secret police – recognises that gaining foreign support requires reform. Gradually loosens political controls; begins Taiwanisation of the regime
- **1986** – Democratic Progressive Party (DPP) established as the first opposition party. Chiang Ching-kuo decides against dissolving it
- **1987** – Martial law lifted after 40 years. Family visits to mainland China allowed
- **1988** – Chiang Ching-kuo dies; succeeded by native Taiwanese Lee Teng-hui as president – first non-mainlander to hold the post
- **1996** – First direct presidential election. Lee Teng-hui wins. China fires missiles into the sea and conducts mock invasion exercises; US responds by sending two aircraft carrier battle groups to the area

### First Democratic Transfer of Power
- **March 2000** – DPP candidate Chen Shui-bian wins presidency with 39% of the vote (KMT vote split between Lien Chan and James Soong). Ends 55 years of KMT rule. Beijing's dream ticket falls apart because Lien and Soong harbour deep personal dislikes
- **Chen's inaugural speech** – promises not to declare independence unless China attacks; not to change the name "Republic of China"; not to hold a referendum on status. More conciliatory than Lee Teng-hui in some ways, but Beijing refuses to engage
- **Beijing miscalculates** – courts the KMT, believing mainlander conservatives will negotiate unification. Misunderstands the nature of Taiwan's political transformation

---

## Modern Taiwan (2000–present)

- **2001** – DPP becomes the largest party in the legislature (with support from Lee Teng-hui's new Taiwan Solidarity Union), though still without a majority. Chen begins "de-Sinicisation" policies: promoting Minnan dialect, "rectification of names," teaching Taiwanese history separately from mainland context
- **September 2001** – 9/11 attacks shift US priorities. Beijing grasps the opportunity: aligns with Washington on anti-terrorism, gains leverage on Taiwan issue. Chen fails to appreciate the changed dynamic
- **April 2001** – US reconnaissance plane forced down by Chinese jets. Bush approves major arms sale to Taiwan; says the US would do "whatever it took" to defend Taiwan
- **2004** – Chen narrowly re-elected (assassination attempt the day before the election). China deploys ~500 missiles aimed at Taiwan (growing to ~700 by mid-2005)
- **2005** – Pentagon admits Chinese submarine force already a deterrent to swift US intervention in a Taiwan crisis
- **2008** – KMT's Ma Ying-jeou wins presidency (58.5% of the vote) on a platform of friendlier relations with China and economic reforms. Chen Shui-bian later prosecuted for corruption
- **2012** – Ma re-elected; KMT retains legislative majority
- **2014** – Sunflower Movement: students occupy parliament protesting undemocratic KMT methods in pushing through a trade agreement with China without proper debate. Changes the mood of the electorate
- **January 2016** – DPP's Tsai Ing-wen wins presidency with 56% of the vote. DPP wins outright majority in the legislature – first time a non-KMT party achieves this
- **2018** – Local elections: major setback for DPP. KMT wins in Kaohsiung (DPP stronghold for 20 years). Tsai resigns as DPP leader
- **May 2019** – Taiwan becomes the first country in Asia to legalise same-sex marriage
- **January 2020** – Tsai re-elected; DPP wins 61 of 113 legislative seats
- **February 2022** – Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine changes Taiwanese public perceptions of the Chinese threat. Accelerates military reform, new weapons acquisitions, higher military spending, and investment in civil defence
- **August 2022** – Nancy Pelosi visits Taiwan. PLA conducts military exercises around Taiwan, including missile launches that overfly the island
- **January 2024** – William Lai Ching-te (DPP) wins presidential election. No party wins a legislative majority for the first time since 2004: DPP 51 seats, KMT 52 seats, Taiwan People's Party (TPP) 8 seats

## Key Themes & Throughlines

- **Sovereignty question remains unresolved** – No post-WWII treaty explicitly ceded Taiwan to any specific state. The Cairo Declaration was a press release, not a treaty. The 1951 San Francisco Treaty had Japan renounce Taiwan without naming a recipient. US policy: Taiwan's status is "undetermined"
- **Aboriginal dispossession** – Continuous across every ruling regime: Dutch, Zheng, Qing, Japanese, KMT. From majority population to 2% (~350,000) today
- **Pattern of foreign abandonment** – Every time Taiwan's future was in the balance, major powers deserted it: 1895 (no one recognised the Republic of Formosa); 1943–52 (Cairo Declaration never formalised); 1971–79 (derecognised in favour of Beijing)
- **Semiconductor "silicon shield"** – By late 2010s, TSMC and Taiwan's semiconductor industry become globally dominant, seen by many as a strategic shield protecting the country from Chinese invasion
- **China's position** – Sustained economic growth is vital to maintenance of internal order and the unchallenged authority of the CCP. "Reunification" with Taiwan is framed as a core national interest. Beijing has never controlled Taiwan
- **Taiwan's identity** – Forged through successive colonial experiences (Dutch, Zheng, Qing, Japanese, KMT mainland exile government). Each regime contributed layers to a distinct Taiwanese identity separate from mainland Chinese identity
